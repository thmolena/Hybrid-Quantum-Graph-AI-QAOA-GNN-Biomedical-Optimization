Graph Neural Networks for QAOA

Graph Neural Networks for the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA)

GNN methods for improving QAOA performance, parameter selection, and scalability across structured graph families — with downstream applications to network-based biomedical systems. This research addresses the classical parameter optimization bottleneck of QAOA through statistical learning across graph-structured optimization instances, enabling uncertainty-aware prediction and generalization under limited data. Figures, notebooks, and the manuscript emphasize downstream evaluation of graph-generated decision variables and transferability across graph families.

Primary Results

Quantitative evaluation of GNN-based QAOA parameterization on held-out transcriptomic graphs, and GNN-based clinical risk scoring on the CTG patient cohort.

The optimization branch evaluates whether a GNN trained on structured graph instances can replace classical parameter search for QAOA with near-parity quality and a strong inference-cost reduction. The biomedical branch demonstrates the generality of the GNN methodology on a clinical node-classification task, consistent with the broader impact on network-based biomedical systems.

How to read these results — metric guide & directionality

Approximation Ratio (QAOA, range 0–1)

Higher = better. Fraction of the theoretically optimal MaxCut value recovered. 0.8682 = 86.82% of the best possible cut. In the table, a −value ↓ opposite a competitor means their score is lower than ours — our model wins. A +value ↑ means that method scores higher, but only through exhaustive search (256 circuit evaluations or full Nelder–Mead at 675 ms).

Accuracy (CTG, range 0–100%)

Higher = better. % of 426 test patients correctly classified. 98.8% = only 5 misclassified.

Balanced Accuracy (range 0–1)

Higher = better. Average recall across both classes — cannot be inflated by predicting "normal" for everyone. Only 8.2% of patients are pathologic, so this is the decisive metric under class imbalance.

ROC AUC (range 0–1)

Higher = better. Probability the model ranks a pathologic exam above a normal one. 0.978 = near-perfect risk separation. Perfect = 1.0.

TP / 35 (True Positives) & FP

TP: higher = better. Dangerous fetuses correctly flagged. Missing one is a serious undetected outcome. FP: lower = better. Patients falsely alarmed. FP = 1 means only one unnecessary clinical intervention on the entire test set.

What the prediction means

The demo samples a graph G = (V, E), builds a simple node-feature view from the graph structure, and then asks the model for a good depth-1 QAOA parameter pair (γ, β). In practical terms, the GNN is learning a shortcut from graph structure to quantum-circuit settings, so it can propose angles without rerunning a full classical search loop for every new graph.

The website currently fixes p = 1 so the interface stays fast, the optimization problem remains easy to interpret, and the frontend stays consistent with the current backend model, which predicts one (γ, β) pair rather than a longer sequence of angles.

The value reported as Expected Cut is the probability-weighted average number of graph edges cut by the final depth-1 QAOA state for the displayed angles. When a reachable /predict endpoint is available, those angles are proposed by the trained GNN backend; otherwise the page computes a browser-side depth-1 QAOA baseline using exact state simulation together with a lightweight local refinement step.

MaxCut Objective
C ( z ) = ∑ ( u , v ) ∈ E 1 - zu zv 2

Interpretation: look at every edge in the graph one by one. If its two endpoints end up on opposite sides of the partition, that edge contributes 1. If they land on the same side, it contributes 0. Add those edge contributions together, and you get the cut value.

Depth-1 QAOA State
| γ , β ⟩ = UB (β) UC (γ) | + ⟩ ⊗ n

Interpretation: start from a state that treats all possible graph partitions equally. Then apply one cost step controlled by γ, which biases the state toward better cuts, and one mixer step controlled by β, which redistributes probability across candidate solutions. That final quantum state is the one evaluated by the demo.

Quantity Being Maximized
⟨ C ⟩ = ⟨ γ , β | C ^ | γ , β ⟩

Interpretation: this is the average cut score produced by the final QAOA state. The algorithm assigns probabilities to many possible graph partitions, computes each partition's cut value, and then takes the probability-weighted average. Better angles are the ones that push more probability onto high-cut solutions, which makes this expected value larger.

Scope & Limitations

  • The website demo is a depth-1 presentation layer; the strongest optimization evidence comes from notebook-level depth-2 evaluations
  • QAOA simulation is exact statevector and therefore remains limited to small graphs because runtime scales exponentially
  • The biomedical results are retrospective research outputs, not a clinically validated deployment system
  • The residual biomedical model provides the strongest held-out result, while the benchmark model is retained for reproducibility and robustness analysis
