UQ-QAOA

Uncertainty-Calibrated Trust Regions for Query-Efficient QAOA Parameter Search

For low-depth implementations of the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm, the number of objective-function queries needed to locate useful variational parameters is the dominant practical cost. UQ-QAOA converts graph-conditioned predictive uncertainty into an operational trust-region geometry: a Graph Isomorphism Network predicts a diagonal Gaussian over QAOA angles, and four information sources are fused by precision-weighted combination into a posterior mean and anisotropic step sizes for sequential coordinate refinement.

Background and Motivation

The scientific setting and the open problem

QAOA is a variational quantum algorithm for combinatorial optimization in which a parameterized circuit is tuned by a classical outer loop. In near-term, measurement-limited execution, the first-class resource is not wall-clock time but the objective-query count — the number of distinct angle vectors evaluated on the quantum processor or simulator.

The resource-aware question

Given a fixed objective-query budget, can graph-conditioned uncertainty allocate those queries more effectively than single-source warm starts or generic black-box optimizers? Barren plateaus and noise-induced effects render gradient-based outer loops inefficient at low depth, so reducing evaluations is a central algorithmic objective.

The structural premise that makes learning possible

For fixed depth and bounded-degree graph families, QAOA parameters exhibit concentration and transferability across related instances. A learned trust-region policy exploits this transferable structure while avoiding unnecessary exploration. The novelty is not that a GNN predicts angles — that capability exists — but that the predicted covariance is repurposed as operational search geometry.

Mathematical Preliminaries

Formal objects and notation

The reader should command the following objects, transcribed from the manuscript.

MaxCut cost Hamiltonian on \(G=(V,E)\), \(n=|V|\)
\[ H_C(G)=\sum_{(i,j)\in E}\frac{1-Z_iZ_j}{2}, \qquad H_M=\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i. \]
QAOA state and exact objective at depth \(p\), \(\theta=(\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_p,\beta_1,\dots,\beta_p)\in\Theta_p\)
\[ \ket{\psi_G(\theta)}=\prod_{\ell=1}^{p}e^{-i\beta_\ell H_M}e^{-i\gamma_\ell H_C(G)}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}, \qquad f_G(\theta)=\bra{\psi_G(\theta)}H_C(G)\ket{\psi_G(\theta)}. \]

Parameter domain \(\Theta_p=[0,2\pi)^p\times[0,\pi)^p\subset\mathbb{R}^{2p}\); approximation ratio \(r_G(\theta)=f_G(\theta)/C_G^\star\).

Finite-shot estimator (cost of one query is one objective evaluation)
\[ \widehat{f}_{G,S}(\theta)=\frac1S\sum_{a=1}^{S}C_G(z_a), \qquad z_a\sim|\braket{z_a}{\psi_G(\theta)}|^2, \qquad Q=\#\{\theta\text{ evaluated}\}. \]
Graph-conditioned predictive distribution (GIN head) and induced trust region
\[ q_\phi(\theta\mid G)=\mathcal{N}\!\big(\mu_\phi(G),\Sigma_\phi(G)\big), \quad \mathcal{T}_{\phi,\rho}(G)=\Big\{\theta\in\Theta_p:\ (\theta-\mu_\phi(G))^{\!\mathsf{T}}(\Sigma_\phi(G)+\lambda I)^{-1}(\theta-\mu_\phi(G))\le\rho^2\Big\}. \]
Precision-weighted four-source posterior (GIN′, local k-NN, global, TQA)
\[ \Sigma_{\mathrm{post}}^{-1}={\Sigma_{\mathrm{GIN}}'}^{-1}+\Sigma_{\mathrm{loc}}^{-1}+\Sigma_{\mathrm{glob}}^{-1}+\Sigma_{\mathrm{TQA}}^{-1}, \qquad \delta_j=\mathrm{clip}\!\big(0.5\sqrt{[\Sigma_{\mathrm{post}}]_{jj}},\,0.05,\,0.30\big). \]
Trotterized quantum annealing prior (physics-informed, test-instance-free)
\[ \gamma_\ell^{\mathrm{TQA}}=\frac{\ell}{p}\,\Delta t,\qquad \beta_\ell^{\mathrm{TQA}}=\Big(1-\frac{\ell}{p}\Big)\Delta t,\qquad \Delta t=T/p. \]
Problem Formulation

Precise problem statement

Fix QAOA depth \(p\) and an objective-query budget \(Q\). For a test graph \(G\), a probabilistic predictor \(h_\phi:G\mapsto(\mu_\phi(G),\Sigma_\phi(G))\) induces a Gaussian over angles and an ellipsoidal trust region \(\mathcal{T}_{\phi,\rho}(G)\). The objective is the budgeted local search

\[ \max_{\theta\in\mathcal{T}_{\phi,\rho}(G)} f_G(\theta)\quad\text{using at most }Q\text{ objective queries,} \]

evaluated by the query-performance curve \(Q\mapsto\max_{1\le q\le Q}r_G(\theta_q)\) and by the query count \(Q_G(\tau)=\min\{q:r_G^{\mathrm{best}}(q)\ge\tau\}\) needed to reach a target ratio \(\tau\). The predicted mean is the warm start; the covariance constructs the local search region and the per-coordinate step sizes — converting a learned distributional object into where to center, how wide to search per coordinate, and how to distribute the budget.

Principal Contributions

Complete claims

  1. Uncertainty as search geometry. Graph-conditioned covariance is used not as a confidence score but as the metric defining anisotropic trust-region scales and the step sizes of coordinate refinement, converting a learned distribution into three operational decisions: where to center, how wide to search per coordinate, and how to distribute a fixed query budget.
  2. Posterior fusion of learned and physics-informed priors. A GIN predictor, a local \(k{=}5\)-nearest-neighbour prior, a global population prior, and a TQA prior are combined into one posterior mean and diagonal covariance via an explicit, non-iterative precision-weighted formula.
  3. Controlled matched- and dimension-scaled-budget evidence. The main benchmark uses \(p{=}3\) with matched query curves; \(p{=}4\)–7 experiments use dimension-scaled budgets \(Q_p=5+4p\). All evaluation is exact-statevector on unweighted MaxCut; claims are bounded to this CPU-only evidence.
  4. Fair local-search baselines and ablations. TQA is run under the identical greedy coordinate-refinement protocol, alongside ablations that remove covariance, shuffle covariance, remove individual priors, or disable refinement, plus black-box optimizers (DE, CMA-ES, GP-EI, Nelder–Mead) under the same budget.
  5. Calibration and finite-shot diagnostics. Because uncertainty is the central object, the work reports trust-region coverage, calibration diagnostics, and finite-shot / noisy-objective sensitivity.
  6. Auditable computational artifact. Deterministic Python scripts generate every number; a C++20 reference interface records the evaluator contract, compiler settings, and kernel structure, separating candidate generation from evaluator kernels for future accelerated backends.

Theoretical Guarantees

Key statements with one-line interpretations

Theorem 1 (Exact-query best-of-\(K\) guarantee). With samples drawn from a proposal on \(\mathcal{T}_{\phi,\rho}(G)\) placing mass \(\alpha_G(\varepsilon)>0\) on the \(\varepsilon\)-optimal subset, if \(K\ge\log(1/\delta)/\alpha_G(\varepsilon)\) then with probability \(\ge1-\delta\), \(f_G(\widehat\theta_K)\ge f_G^{\mathcal{T}}-\varepsilon\); more precisely \(\Pr[f_G(\widehat\theta_K)Interpretation: a calibrated trust region helps only insofar as it concentrates proposal mass on near-optimal angles; query efficiency is governed by \(\alpha_G(\varepsilon)\).
Theorem 2 (Finite-shot best-of-\(K\) guarantee). Under sub-Gaussian shot noise of proxy variance \(\sigma_G^2/S\) with \(\sigma_G^2\le|E|^2/4\), choosing \(K\ge\log(2/\delta)/\alpha_G(\varepsilon/2)\) and \(S\ge 32\sigma_G^2\log(4K/\delta)/\varepsilon^2\) yields \(f_G(\widehat\theta_K^{\mathrm{shot}})\ge f_G^{\mathcal{T}}-\varepsilon\) w.p. \(\ge1-\delta\).
Interpretation: parameter-query cost and shot cost separate — few queries can place good candidates while many shots may be needed to compare them reliably.
Proposition 1 (Finite-sample conformal coverage). With exchangeable validation/test instances and Mahalanobis nonconformity scores, the conformal radius \(\hat q_{1-\alpha}\) gives \(\mathbb{P}[\theta^*(G_{M+1})\in\mathcal{T}_{\hat q_{1-\alpha}}(G_{M+1})]\ge1-\alpha\).
Interpretation: the trust-region radius admits a distribution-free finite-sample coverage guarantee, available as a calibration option.
Proposition 2 (Optimality of precision-weighted fusion). If each source reports \(\mu_s=\theta^\star+\varepsilon_s\), \(\varepsilon_s\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_s)\) independent, then among linear unbiased estimators \(\sum_s W_s\mu_s\) with \(\sum_s W_s=I\), the precision weights \(W_s^\star=\Sigma_{\mathrm{post}}\Sigma_s^{-1}\) uniquely minimize the error covariance in the Loewner order, attaining \(\Sigma_{\mathrm{post}}\).
Interpretation: the fusion rule is the unique minimum-variance unbiased combiner (and the flat-prior posterior mean), certifying the tightest unbiased center.
Lemma 1 (Per-coordinate trust-region contraction). For diagonal sources, \([\Sigma_{\mathrm{post}}]_{jj}\le\min_s[\Sigma_s]_{jj}\) for every coordinate \(j\); hence the fused step size never exceeds that of the most confident single source, and the fused axis-aligned region is contained in every source’s.
Interpretation: fusion never enlarges the per-coordinate search scale — the smaller volume enters Theorem 1 through \(\det(\Sigma_\phi+\lambda I)\), reducing required queries.
Proposition 3 (Convergence of greedy coordinate refinement). With \(f_G\) bounded (\(0\le f_G\le|E|\)), the incumbent value sequence of Phase 2c is nondecreasing and converges; a pass without strict improvement yields a \(\delta\)-approximate coordinate-wise local maximum, and step-halving drives \(\delta\to0\) so every limit point is coordinate-wise stationary wherever \(f_G\) is differentiable.
Interpretation: the refinement loop is well-posed and converges to a coordinate-wise stationary incumbent under the trust-region contraction rule.

Significance and Impact

Consequence for the field and downstream application

Consequence

UQ-QAOA establishes that graph-conditioned predictive uncertainty is itself a test-time resource-allocation object: the learned covariance constructs the local search geometry and the dimension-wise step sizes, connecting initialization quality to query-budget distribution through an explicit, theoretically certified geometric mechanism. The method requires no gradient or Hessian estimates from the quantum backend and maintains no per-instance GP surrogate — the query budget is spent directly on objective evaluation.

Stated limitations (kept visible)

  • Evidence is exact-statevector, CPU-only, on unweighted MaxCut at small \(n\le18\); no hardware or quantum-advantage claims are made.
  • The four sources are not strictly independent (k-NN and GIN share training data); fusion is an engineering approximation, justified empirically by the ablation.
  • Covariance is diagonal; cross-layer angle correlations and torus topology of \(\Theta_p\) are not modeled (wrapped-normal / full-covariance extensions are future work).
  • At \(p{=}6,7\) with a minimal single-instance budget (Q=5) methods are tightly clustered; statistical claims at \(p>3\) are explicitly not made.

Determinism is enforced by an explicit backend contract: fixed candidate ordering by seed and method, duplicate-anchor removal before budget expenditure, modular wrapping of out-of-domain angles, exactly-once budget decrement per unique evaluation, and a fixed floating-point reduction policy.
