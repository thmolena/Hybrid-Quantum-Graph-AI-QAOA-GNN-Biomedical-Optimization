0. Theoretical Foundation: Why Hybrid Quantum-Classical? ¶

The central idea¶

The notebook does not treat quantum and classical computation as competing replacements. It treats them as complementary components inside one workflow. The classical side organizes data, chooses parameters, and evaluates progress. The quantum-style routine supplies a structured search mechanism for the optimization task. That division of labor is what makes the pipeline hybrid.

Why a hybrid design is the realistic starting point¶

Current quantum hardware remains limited in depth, noise tolerance, and reliability. In practical terms, that means:

  • circuits must remain relatively short,
  • repeated measurements introduce uncertainty,
  • and classical hardware still performs most surrounding optimization and data management work more effectively.

For presentation purposes, the most accurate mental model is this: the classical computer orchestrates the workflow, while the quantum routine acts as a specialized optimization module.

The feedback loop¶

         ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
         │  Classical computer             │
         │  chooses QAOA parameters        │
         │  and evaluates progress         │
         └──────────────┬──────────────────┘
                        │  send (gamma, beta)
         ┌──────────────┼──────────────────┐
         │  Quantum routine                │
         │  prepares the circuit,          │
         │  evaluates the candidate        │
         │  and returns a score            │
         └──────────────┬──────────────────┘
                        │  return expected cut
                        └──> update parameters

What QAOA contributes in this notebook¶

QAOA, the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm, is used here as a parameterized search procedure for MaxCut. In operational terms, we:

  • start from a simple reference state,
  • apply problem-informed transformations,
  • apply mixing transformations that preserve exploration,
  • evaluate the resulting state,
  • and update the angles $(\gamma, \beta)$ to improve the score.

Those angles are the key quantities learned, optimized, and later visualized throughout the quantum section.

Mathematical statement¶

The general objective in variational quantum algorithms is often written as

$$\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol\theta) = \langle\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)|\hat{H}|\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)\rangle$$

For readers who prefer intuition over notation, the interpretation is straightforward:

  • $\boldsymbol\theta$ are tunable parameters,
  • $|\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)\rangle$ is the state created by those parameters,
  • $\hat{H}$ is the quantity we care about,
  • and the expression returns a score to optimize.

In this notebook, the tunable parameters are $(\gamma, \beta)$ and the score is the expected cut value for MaxCut.

Why introduce a GNN into the loop¶

Optimizing QAOA from scratch for every new graph can be repetitive and costly. A graph neural network offers a learned shortcut: it receives the graph structure and predicts a strong initial parameter pair $(\hat\gamma, \hat\beta)$.

Conceptually, the transition is from

optimizing each graph independently

to

learning regularities across many graphs so that new instances can be handled more efficiently.

Why graphs appear in both halves of the notebook¶

Graphs are the unifying language of the project:

  1. In the QAOA section, the graph is the optimization problem.
  2. In the biomedical section, the graph captures patient similarity.

That is the main conceptual bridge of the notebook: graph structure supports both optimization and prediction, even though the downstream tasks are different.

Part 1: QAOA for a Graph Optimization Problem ¶

Problem statement: MaxCut¶

We begin with a graph and ask a clean optimization question: can we partition the nodes into two sets so that as many edges as possible cross between the sets? That objective is the MaxCut problem.

A useful non-technical interpretation is that the graph contains relationships, and we seek a split that separates as many connected pairs as possible.

Why MaxCut is an effective benchmark¶

MaxCut is widely used in optimization and quantum-computing demonstrations because it is:

  • easy to define clearly,
  • naturally expressed through binary decisions,
  • and still rich enough to reveal how variational optimization behaves.

Quantum formulation¶

In QAOA, the optimization target is encoded in the cost Hamiltonian

$$\hat{C} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{(u,v)\in E}(I - Z_u Z_v)$$

The operational meaning is more important than the notation: each edge contributes to the objective, separated endpoints increase the score, and the quantum state is evaluated according to that total score.

The two alternating ingredients¶

QAOA alternates between two types of transformations:

  1. Cost step
    This step encodes the structure of the MaxCut objective into the state evolution.
  2. Mixer step
    This step preserves exploration by moving probability mass across candidate solutions.

At depth $p=1$, the model uses one cost angle and one mixer angle:

  • $\gamma$ controls the problem-dependent evolution,
  • $\beta$ controls the mixing behavior.

State preparation and scoring¶

The variational state is

$$|\gamma, \beta\rangle = U_B(\beta)\,U_C(\gamma)\,|+\rangle^{\otimes n}$$

In practical terms, the procedure is:

  • start from an unbiased reference state,
  • apply a transformation tied to the optimization objective,
  • apply a transformation that preserves exploration,
  • and evaluate the expected cut value.

Why the exact solution is still useful here¶

Because the demonstration graph is intentionally small, we can compute the exact MaxCut value by brute force. That gives us a rigorous reference point:

  • Exact MaxCut: the best attainable cut for this graph.
  • QAOA expected cut: the average score produced by a chosen parameter pair.

The difference between these two quantities tells us how competitive the QAOA parameters are.

  1. it constructs the graph instance,
  2. it computes the exact MaxCut reference,
  3. it runs a classical optimization loop to identify strong QAOA angles.

Those optimized angles become the baseline that the learned graph model will later try to approximate.

# CELL 1: Generate a MaxCut graph and optimize QAOA parameters
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

import time, math

p = 1   # QAOA depth — only 2 variational parameters (γ₁, β₁)
n = 6   # qubits / graph vertices  (2^6 = 64 amplitudes)

# ── Sample an Erdős–Rényi random graph G(n, p_edge=0.5, seed=7)
# E[m] = p_edge * C(n,2) = 0.5 * 15 = 7.5 edges
G_nx = sample_erdos_renyi(n, p_edge=0.5, seed=7)
edges = list(G_nx.edges())

print(f'MaxCut graph: n={n}, m={len(edges)}')
print(f'Edges: {edges}')
print(f'Degree sequence: {sorted([d for _, d in G_nx.degree()], reverse=True)}')
print(f'Is connected: {nx.is_connected(G_nx)}')

# ── Brute-force MaxCut (exact, O(2^n * m))
best_cut = 0
best_partition = 0
for mask in range(1, 2**n):
    cut = sum(1 for (u, v) in edges
              if ((mask >> u) & 1) != ((mask >> v) & 1))
    if cut > best_cut:
        best_cut = cut
        best_partition = mask
print(f'\nExact MaxCut  : {best_cut}')
print(f"Best partition: {format(best_partition, f'0{n}b')}  "
      f"(S={[i for i in range(n) if (best_partition >> i) & 1]}, "
      f"S̄={[i for i in range(n) if not (best_partition >> i) & 1]})")

# ── Classical QAOA optimization: minimize -⟨C⟩(γ, β) via Nelder–Mead
def classical_optimize(n, edges, p, maxiter=300, seed=SEED):
    """
    Nelder–Mead minimization of the negative QAOA expected cut.


    The parameter space is [0, π]^p × [0, π/2]^p.
    At p=1, this is a 2D unconstrained optimization.
    """
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)

    def neg_exp(x):
        g, b = x[:p], x[p:]
        return -expected_cut(n, edges, qaoa_state(n, edges, g, b))

    x0 = rng.uniform(0, math.pi, size=2 * p)

    res = minimize(
        neg_exp,
        x0,
        method='Nelder-Mead',
        options={'maxiter': maxiter, 'xatol': 1e-5, 'fatol': 1e-5},
    )
    return res.x[:p], res.x[p:], -res.fun

t0 = time.perf_counter()
opt_g, opt_b, val_opt = classical_optimize(n, edges, p)
classical_time = time.perf_counter() - t0

print(f'\nClassical QAOA Optimization (Nelder–Mead, p={p})')
print(f'  γ_opt   = {opt_g}')
print(f'  β_opt   = {opt_b}')
print(f'  ⟨C⟩_opt = {val_opt:.4f}  (exact MaxCut = {best_cut})')
print(f'  Approx ratio: {val_opt / best_cut:.4f}')
print(f'  Time    = {classical_time * 1000:.2f} ms')

# ── Save artifacts
pd.DataFrame(edges, columns=['u', 'v']).to_csv(
    os.path.join(proj_root, 'outputs', 'maxcut_graph.csv'), index=False)
pd.DataFrame({'opt_gamma': opt_g, 'opt_beta': opt_b}).to_csv(
    os.path.join(proj_root, 'outputs', 'qaoa_classical_angles.csv'), index=False)
print('\nSaved: outputs/maxcut_graph.csv, outputs/qaoa_classical_angles.csv')

Part 2: GNN Parameter Prediction ¶

The computational bottleneck¶

Part 1 optimized QAOA angles for a single graph. If that procedure must be repeated for many graph instances, the cost accumulates quickly. The central question of this section is therefore: can a graph model infer useful QAOA settings directly from graph structure?

Intuition before the formalism¶

Graphs with related structural patterns often benefit from related QAOA parameters. A graph neural network is well suited to learning this relationship because it was designed to process graph topology rather than independent tabular rows.

In concise terms:

  • the classical optimizer learns one instance at a time,
  • the GNN attempts to learn a transferable rule across instances.

Why this matters¶

If the GNN is successful, then for a new graph the workflow becomes:

  • encode the graph,
  • run one forward pass,
  • obtain predicted values for $(\hat\gamma, \hat\beta)$,
  • evaluate those parameters inside QAOA.

That is attractive because it replaces a repeated search loop with a learned approximation.

What the model receives¶

This notebook uses a lightweight GCN-style model with deliberately simple inputs:

  • the adjacency structure of the graph,
  • and a node feature derived from degree.

This design choice keeps the notebook readable while still capturing the essential hybrid idea: graph structure can inform parameter prediction.

Core message-passing rule¶

A graph convolution layer can be written as

$$\mathbf{H}^{(l+1)} = \text{ReLU}\!\left(\hat{D}^{-1/2}\hat{A}\hat{D}^{-1/2}\,\mathbf{H}^{(l)}\,\mathbf{W}^{(l)}\right)$$

Operationally, this means that each node updates its representation using information from its local neighborhood, and repeated layers turn local signals into a graph-level summary.

What the output means¶

The GNN does not solve MaxCut directly. It predicts the QAOA control parameters that will later be evaluated by the optimization routine. The pipeline is therefore:

  1. graph enters the GNN,
  2. the GNN predicts $(\hat\gamma, \hat\beta)$,
  3. those angles are passed into QAOA,
  4. the resulting expected cut is compared with the classical baseline.

Why simple features are still informative¶

Degree alone is an intentionally modest feature, but it already captures whether a graph is sparse or dense, homogeneous or uneven, and whether certain nodes act as local hubs. For an educational demonstration, that is a reasonable balance between interpretability and relevance.

What to watch for in the next code cell¶

The next cell loads the graph model, prepares the graph tensors, and compares three quantities:

  • the classically optimized angle pair,
  • the GNN-predicted angle pair,
  • and the expected cut induced by each choice.

The scientific question is not whether the angles are numerically identical. The more important question is whether the predicted angles produce a competitive cut value at substantially lower latency.

Part 3: Expected-Cut Landscape and Parameter Heatmap ¶

Why visualize the landscape¶

Once QAOA parameters have been identified, the next question is how sensitive performance is to those choices. Plotting $\langle C\rangle(\gamma, \beta)$ over a grid of angle pairs reveals the response surface of the optimization problem.

A useful interpretation is:

  • high regions correspond to stronger parameter choices,
  • low regions correspond to weaker parameter choices,
  • peaks or bright bands reveal where optimization is most successful.

Why this plot matters¶

The landscape clarifies several points that single summary numbers cannot show on their own:

  • whether the search space is smooth or irregular,
  • whether good performance lies in one dominant region or several competing regions,
  • whether the GNN prediction lands in a useful neighborhood even if it is not the exact optimum.

Meaning of the axes¶

  • $\gamma$ controls how strongly the circuit reflects the MaxCut objective.
  • $\beta$ controls how strongly the circuit mixes across candidate solutions.

At depth $p=1$, these two angles completely determine the behavior of the variational circuit in this notebook.

Why a numerical simulator is appropriate here¶

For very small cases, parts of the QAOA landscape can be analyzed in closed form. In this notebook we use a direct numerical simulator instead. That choice keeps the analysis general, transparent, and consistent with the other computational sections.

What would count as a strong learned prediction¶

A strong prediction does not need to hit the exact global optimum. It needs to place the evaluation in a region where the expected cut remains high. In practice, the distinction matters because rapid access to a high-quality region can be more useful than spending additional time to find the mathematically best point.

What the next figure is designed to show¶

The figure below combines three complementary views:

  1. the graph instance itself,
  2. a one-dimensional slice through the landscape,
  3. the full two-dimensional heatmap.

Together they show not only what the best parameters are, but also how forgiving or sensitive the surrounding region is.

Interpreting the QAOA landscape figure¶

Left panel — graph instance
This panel shows the MaxCut problem itself. Node size scales with degree, so visually prominent nodes are the ones that influence many edges. It establishes the structure over which the optimization routine is operating.

Middle panel — one-dimensional slice through the landscape
Here only $\gamma$ is varied while $\beta$ is held fixed at the classical optimum. This simplifies the interpretation by showing how sharply or smoothly the score responds to a single control parameter. A broad high region indicates tolerance to small parameter errors; a narrow peak indicates that performance is more sensitive.

Right panel — full two-dimensional response surface
The heatmap shows the expected cut value for many $(\gamma, \beta)$ combinations. Bright regions correspond to stronger parameter pairs. The overlaid markers show where the classical optimizer and the GNN prediction land within that surface.

What readers should look for¶

  • If the bright region is broad, the problem is forgiving and approximate predictions can still perform well.
  • If the bright region is concentrated, accurate parameter selection matters more.
  • If the GNN marker lies close to a bright region, the model has learned a structurally meaningful shortcut even when it does not coincide exactly with the classical optimum.

This visualization is the bridge between the optimization output and the learned predictor. It shows whether the GNN is merely producing arbitrary numbers or whether it is locating the part of parameter space that carries high optimization value.

Part 4: Biomedical GCN — Graph-Structured Fetal Monitoring Risk Classification ¶

Section summary¶

The optimization branch of the notebook uses a graph as the problem to be solved. The biomedical branch uses a graph as the representation of relationships among physiological monitoring exams. Each node corresponds to one cardiotocography exam and each edge links exams with similar measurement patterns. The task in this section is binary risk classification: pathologic versus non-pathologic fetal state.

Dataset and cohort design¶

The biomedical analysis uses the UCI Cardiotocography dataset, a real fetal-monitoring cohort derived from heart-rate and uterine-contraction recordings. The source dataset contains 2,126 exams and 21 physiologic summary features, which makes it substantially larger and more clinically representative than a small benchmark expansion.

Cohort element Description

Cohort size 2,126 exams
Features 21 physiologic summary measurements
Original labels normal, suspect, pathologic
Analytical target binary risk framing: pathologic vs non-pathologic
Role in this notebook a real biological cohort for graph-based risk classification

Scope note¶

The binary framing is a modeling choice for risk-sensitive analysis, not a claim that the source dataset is inherently binary. The original obstetric labels are preserved in the saved tables, while the graph model is optimized for the more clinically consequential distinction between pathologic and non-pathologic exams.

Why a graph model is used here¶

A conventional classifier treats each exam largely as an independent row. A graph model adds relational structure: exams with similar physiologic profiles can influence one another’s learned representation. That is useful when higher-risk cases occupy overlapping regions of feature space rather than forming a perfectly isolated cluster.

Model logic¶

The model builds an exam-similarity graph with k-nearest neighbors and then updates each exam representation by combining:

  • the exam’s own feature vector,
  • and information aggregated from nearby exams in the graph.

The resulting workflow is:

  • standardize the physiologic measurements using training-set statistics only,
  • construct the similarity graph across the cohort,
  • propagate information across local neighborhoods,
  • output a risk prediction for each exam node.

Mathematical form¶

$$\text{Input: }\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times 21}, \quad \hat{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$$

$$\mathbf{H}^{(1)} = \text{ReLU}(\hat{A}_{\rm norm}\,\mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{W}^{(0)})$$

$$\mathbf{H}^{(2)} = \text{ReLU}(\hat{A}_{\rm norm}\,\mathbf{H}^{(1)}\,\mathbf{W}^{(1)})$$

$$\hat{y}_i = \text{softmax}\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{(2)}_i\,\mathbf{W}^{(2)}\right)$$

Why this section belongs in the same notebook¶

The connection to the QAOA section is structural:

  • both branches use graphs as the central representation,
  • both use graph neural methods to exploit that structure,
  • and both show how learned models can strengthen a larger computational workflow.

The optimization branch focuses on parameter selection. The biomedical branch focuses on predictive inference. Together they show two complementary uses of graph learning.

class BioGCN(nn.Module):
    """
    Two-layer GCN for transductive node classification on the CTG graph.
    """

    def __init__(self, in_dim: int, hidden: int, n_classes: int):
        super().__init__()
        self.fc1 = nn.Linear(in_dim, hidden, bias=False)
        self.fc2 = nn.Linear(hidden, hidden // 2, bias=False)
        self.clf = nn.Linear(hidden // 2, n_classes)
        self.drop = nn.Dropout(p=0.20)

    def forward(self, A: torch.Tensor, X: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        h = F.relu(A @ self.fc1(X))
        h = self.drop(h)
        h = F.relu(A @ self.fc2(h))
        h = self.drop(h)
        return self.clf(h)

Part 5: Reading the Evaluation Dashboard ¶

The evaluation dashboard is designed to answer a practical presentation question: is the biomedical graph model separating clinically higher-risk CTG exams from the broader cohort in a credible and interpretable way?

Top row — predictive performance¶

Confusion matrix
This panel shows the exact counts of correct and incorrect predictions on the held-out CTG test cohort. The most important number is the recall for pathologic exams, because that reflects how consistently the model identifies the higher-risk state under the notebook’s binary risk framing.

ROC curve
This panel evaluates discrimination across thresholds rather than at one single decision rule. The curve is plotted with pathologic detection treated as the positive outcome, which keeps the interpretation aligned with risk-sensitive screening.

Learning dynamics
This panel shows how training and validation accuracy evolve relative to validation loss. A stable pattern is one in which validation accuracy remains competitive while validation loss improves or stabilizes, indicating that the selected model is not drifting away from the held-out validation signal.

Bottom row — geometry and interpretability¶

PCA projection with true labels
This panel compresses the 21-dimensional monitoring space into two principal components. It provides a global view of how much the pathologic and non-pathologic cohorts overlap before considering the graph model.

PCA projection with predicted labels
This panel shows where the model places its class decisions in the same low-dimensional space. The black outlines indicate held-out test exams, and the highlighted discordant cases show where the model’s predictions diverge from the held-out labels.

Feature-shift panel
This panel summarizes which standardized physiologic measurements differ most strongly between the pathologic and non-pathologic training cohorts. It is a descriptive view of the cohort rather than a full mechanistic explanation of the GCN.

How to summarize the figure aloud¶

A concise presentation summary would be: the graph model captures most of the higher-risk CTG exams, maintains strong overall discrimination, and concentrates the remaining held-out discordances in the more ambiguous parts of the cohort geometry.

Synthesis: How QAOA and Graph Learning Connect ¶

Concise synthesis¶

This notebook develops one central argument across two application settings: graph structure is a powerful organizing principle for both optimization and prediction. In the quantum section, graph learning helps propose useful QAOA parameters. In the biomedical section, graph learning helps classify physiological monitoring exams by using similarity relationships directly.

Side-by-side interpretation¶

Component What the graph represents What the model outputs Primary objective
QAOA + GNN a MaxCut optimization instance predicted QAOA angles $(\hat\gamma, \hat\beta)$ reduce repeated parameter-search cost
Biomedical GCN a physiologic exam-similarity network class probabilities and labels support structured risk classification

Why the notebook is conceptually coherent¶

The notebook is not claiming that the biomedical task is quantum, nor that every graph problem needs a quantum routine. The coherence comes from a more disciplined observation: once a problem is expressed as a graph, learned models can exploit that structure in multiple ways. In one setting they help guide optimization; in another they support classification.

A concise non-technical description¶

A clear summary for a mixed audience is:

This project studies how graph-based AI can support a hybrid quantum optimization workflow while also demonstrating graph-based biomedical classification on a real fetal-monitoring cohort.

That wording is accurate, appropriately scoped, and consistent with what the notebook demonstrates.

Future Directions ¶

Near-term directions¶

  • Enrich the graph features used for QAOA parameter prediction so the GNN can exploit more than degree information.
  • Evaluate the warm-start idea across broader graph families and larger instance collections.
  • Replace the lightweight simulator with a hardware-facing backend for small proof-of-concept comparisons when appropriate.

Medium-term directions¶

  • Extend the optimization analysis beyond MaxCut to additional graph-structured objectives.
  • Measure whether learned parameter initialization reduces the number of classical optimization steps in a statistically robust way.
  • Study how biomedical graph embeddings can support downstream tasks such as cohort stratification or feature subset selection.

Long-term perspective¶

The most realistic long-term outlook is not a replacement narrative in which quantum computation displaces classical machine learning. A more credible direction is selective integration: specialized quantum routines, when practically useful, will sit inside broader classical AI systems. This notebook offers a compact example of what that division of labor can look like.
